The digital landscape in Sri Lanka continues to evolve rapidly, bringing both opportunities and challenges for fundamental rights and freedoms. This policy brief examines recent developments in digital rights legislation and provides recommendations for policymakers and civil society.

## Executive Summary

Sri Lanka's digital ecosystem is at a critical juncture. While internet penetration has grown significantly, reaching over 50% of the population, concerns about digital rights, privacy, and online freedoms have intensified. Recent legislative proposals and government policies have raised questions about the balance between security, regulation, and fundamental freedoms.

## Key Findings

### 1. Privacy and Data Protection

The absence of comprehensive data protection legislation remains a significant gap in Sri Lanka's legal framework. While the Right to Information Act provides some protections, there is no dedicated law governing how personal data is collected, processed, and protected by both public and private entities.

**Recommendations:**
- Enact comprehensive data protection legislation aligned with international standards
- Establish an independent data protection authority
- Ensure meaningful consent mechanisms for data collection

### 2. Online Content Regulation

Recent proposals to regulate online content have raised concerns about potential impacts on freedom of expression. While addressing legitimate concerns about harmful content is important, regulations must be carefully crafted to avoid chilling effects on legitimate speech.

**Key Concerns:**
- Overly broad definitions of prohibited content
- Lack of due process in content removal procedures
- Potential for arbitrary enforcement

### 3. Digital Access and Inclusion

While internet access has expanded, significant digital divides persist based on geography, income, and education. These disparities affect citizens' ability to exercise their digital rights and participate in the digital economy.

## Legislative Developments

### Cybersecurity Act

The proposed Cybersecurity Act has generated significant debate within civil society and the tech sector. While cybersecurity is undoubtedly important, provisions in the current draft raise concerns about:

- Broad surveillance powers
- Lack of judicial oversight mechanisms
- Potential impacts on encryption and secure communications
- Ambiguous definitions that could enable overreach

### Online Safety Bill

Efforts to combat online harassment and abuse are welcome, but proposed online safety legislation must strike the right balance. Key considerations include:

- Clear, specific definitions of prohibited content
- Independent oversight of enforcement
- Appeals mechanisms for content removal decisions
- Protection for intermediaries while maintaining accountability

## International Standards and Best Practices

Sri Lanka can learn from international experiences in digital rights regulation:

### European Union
The GDPR provides a robust framework for data protection while enabling legitimate data processing. Key principles include:
- Purpose limitation
- Data minimization
- Transparency
- Individual rights and remedies

### India
India's journey with data protection legislation offers lessons about stakeholder consultation and balancing competing interests.

### Kenya
Kenya's Data Protection Act demonstrates how developing countries can establish effective data governance frameworks.

## Recommendations for Policy Makers

1. **Conduct Comprehensive Consultations**: Engage civil society, industry, and affected communities in legislative processes

2. **Rights-Based Approach**: Center human rights in all digital policy development

3. **Independent Oversight**: Establish independent bodies to oversee implementation and enforcement

4. **Transparency**: Ensure government surveillance and data collection activities are subject to transparency requirements

5. **Capacity Building**: Invest in digital literacy and rights awareness programs

## The Role of Civil Society

CDPR and civil society organizations have a critical role to play in:

- Monitoring legislative developments
- Providing technical expertise to policymakers
- Educating the public about digital rights
- Documenting rights violations
- Advocating for rights-respecting policies

## Conclusion

Sri Lanka stands at a crossroads in its digital policy journey. The decisions made today will shape the digital landscape for generations to come. By adopting a rights-based approach, conducting meaningful consultations, and learning from international best practices, Sri Lanka can build a digital ecosystem that protects fundamental freedoms while addressing legitimate policy concerns.

CDPR remains committed to working with all stakeholders to ensure that Sri Lanka's digital future is one that respects human rights, promotes innovation, and serves the interests of all citizens.
